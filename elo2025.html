What is Seis8s?

Seis8s (seis8s.org) is a Spanish-inflected, web-based live coding environment designed to foreground Latin electronic dance music traditions, especially cumbia music, through real-time programming of rhythmic and melodic patterns. Seis8s can be accessed at https://seis8s.org/

Seis8s is influenced by traditions of socially conscious Latin dance music ‚Äî what Agurto (2021) describes as ‚Äútexts and musical markers that poetically express political, historical, and class awareness of the shared Latino/Latin American existences, identities, and experiences‚Äù (xix). These forms reflect on migration, poverty, and racism while also producing a sense of joy, resistance, and collective identity through festive rhythms. Examples include Cumbia Villera, a raw and rebellious 1990s Argentine style that addressed economic disparity, and Salsa dura, which in 1970s New York became a vehicle for Afro-Latin resistance through syncopated, improvisatory sound.

Seis8s extends these traditions into the realm of code, to speculate the look and feel of a computer-music language grounded in Spanish language, and addressing an imagined community of users with shared cultural, political, economic, historical, and technological traits.

Seis8s was part of my PhD dissertation entitled Culturally situated programming platforms: seis8s, a live-coding language for electronic latin dance music (2023).

About the visual aesthetic

Seis8s interface intentionally resists sterile UI norms such as Apple‚Äôs Human Interface Guide lines or Google‚Äôs Material Design system. Instead, it draws inspiration from the vivid and chaotic energy of Peruvian Chicha poster art, which is the ‚ÄùPeruvian Kitsch thetic ... born in the 1980s‚Äù that deliberately breaks from modernist design principles, layering bold colors, irregular letterforms, and multiple fonts in ways that feel exuberant and unruly.

The color scheme was derived from the work of Chicha artist Yefferson Huam¬¥an, particularly a poster featured on Movimientos.org[3], which mixes saturated green, orange, and purple tones in asymmetric, high-contrast layouts.

The design process began in Figma, where I composed the interface as a flat graphic using SVGs. Once exported, the SVG file was imported into the Vue.js framework and transformed into an interactive layer‚Äîallowing users to click on buttons, launch sound events, or interact with timeline controls directly on top of the artwork.

The use of SVG as an interface foundation emerged not only from aesthetic goals, but also from pedagogical insight. While teaching a course on design and code in 2023, I came to appreciate SVG‚Äôs expressive power which allows to draw complex layouts, then render them in the browser exactly as envisioned, without having to deconstruct every visual into CSS grids, box models, or flex alignment rules. üü† The G√ºiro Rhythm (20 min)

The g√ºiro is a percussion instrument. It is classified within the idiophone division (which produce sound by themselves, without strings or drumheads), specifically in the scraper family. Traditional g√ºiros are made from dried calabash gourds, similar to other Latin instruments like the cabasa and maracas, which also use this fruit.

This instrument is typical in several Latin American countries, including Ecuador, Brazil, Colombia, the Dominican Republic, Panama, Mexico, Peru, Cuba, Puerto Rico, and Venezuela. It is commonly used as an accompanying rhythm instrument.

Archaeological evidence of scraper instruments resembling the g√ºiro has been found in ancient sites across Mexico, showing the instrument‚Äôs deep cultural roots (Wikipedia).

üîµ The Conga March (20 min)

The conga, also known as tumbadora, is a tall, narrow, single-headed drum from Cuba. Congas are staved like barrels and classified into three types: quinto (lead drum, highest), tres dos or tres golpes (middle), and tumba or salidor (lowest).

Congas were originally used in Afro-Cuban music genres such as conga (hence their name) and rumba, where each drummer would play a single drum. Following numerous innovations in conga drumming and construction during the mid-20th century, as well as its internationalization, it became increasingly common for drummers to play two or three drums.

Congas have become a popular instrument in many forms of Latin music such as son (when played by conjuntos), descarga, Afro-Cuban jazz, salsa, songo, merengue and Latin rock. (Wikipedia).
